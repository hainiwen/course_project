Project description:
Antibiotic resistance in Escherichia coli (E. coli) poses a significant challenge to public health, as the rise of multidrug-resistant strains complicates treatment options for common infections. The development of resistance, particularly to antibiotics such as ampicillin, ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMZ), is influenced heavily by prior antibiotic exposure. Studies indicate that even low-level or sub-inhibitory antibiotic exposure can select for resistant strains, emphasizing the importance of prudent antibiotic use in both clinical and agricultural settings.

Research question:
Does the pattern of prior antibiotic exposure , characterised by both recent exposure presence and antibiotic-free interval duration, influence the rate of susceptibility state transitions in Klebsiella pneumoniae and Escherichia coli isolated from urine cultures, and if so, does the effect magnitude and direction differ by organism, antibiotic exposure type, and transition direction?
